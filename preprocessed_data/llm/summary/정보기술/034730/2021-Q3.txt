- **Global Semiconductor Shortage:**
  - The global automotive market has faced significant supply shortages post-pandemic, with major car-producing countries (excluding China) experiencing a 20% to 40% reduction in production compared to two years ago.
  - The shortage of semiconductors is expected to cause production disruptions affecting over 10 million vehicles this year.

- **U.S. Government's Response:**
  - President Biden convened a virtual meeting with global semiconductor companies to address the shortage.
  - The U.S. Department of Commerce requested detailed information from these companies, including inventory, sales, and customer data, through a 26-question survey.

- **Corporate Compliance and Concerns:**
  - TSMC, the leading semiconductor foundry, submitted the requested information excluding confidential data.
  - South Korean companies Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix also deliberated extensively before submitting their responses, excluding sensitive customer information.

- **Geopolitical Tensions:**
  - The U.S. views semiconductors as a critical security issue, emphasizing technological supremacy.
  - There is concern that the information provided could include data on Chinese production and sales, potentially provoking a backlash from China similar to past retaliations (e.g., THAAD deployment).

- **Economic and Diplomatic Implications:**
  - Approximately 1% of the production from South Korean companies is supplied to China, highlighting the interconnectedness of global supply chains.
  - The U.S. Secretary of Commerce indicated that insufficient data submissions might lead to further actions, potentially invoking the Defense Production Act for additional pressure.

- **Diplomatic Engagements:**
  - South Korea's Minister of Trade, Industry, and Energy plans to meet with the U.S. Secretary of Commerce in Washington to discuss cooperation regarding the data request.
